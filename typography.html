Axial typographic systems divide everything by an axis, here I have divided elements with axis from stretched parts of the letters L and T, as well as an invisible axis which runs between the lines of the title text.

Radial systems have elements extend and radiate from a single point of focus, usually at a perpedicular angle, but for my system I have decided to make the text flow somewhat naturally from the top-left point of focus.

Dilatational systems are similar to radial systems in the way that elements extend from circlular shapes, but instead these elements are usually horizontal.

Random typographic systems have their elements arranged randomly without any any aim, direction or defnite pattern. This can be entertaining for the reader as it creates fun while they search for connections between elements. In my illustration I decided to make the important information still readable in a different colour, but consistently in a random fashion.

Grid systems prioritise text first, making it one of the best systems for readability. All text and graphics in grid systems fit within consistent columns and rows. My design is kept simple, as most grid systems usually are.

Modular systems use repeating patterns and shapes to break up elements and text. It can be similar to the grid typograhy system, but for my illustration I have intentionally not arranged my elements in a grid formation to clearly show a difference.

Transitional typographic systems are mostly arranged in layers of text, in a manner which gives the impression that it has movement or is "flowing" in a direction. Shapes can be used to accentuate the layers in each design, in my case I have sandwiched a paragraph between two coloured spaces to give the impression that it is guiding each tiny stream of text.

Bilateral designs usually are symmetrical and have their text centered, I have tried to make this one unique by having the text still centered but with reshaped the textboxes, additionally with alternating colours to break it up slightly.
